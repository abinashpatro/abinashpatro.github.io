A New Methodology for Mesh Regularization Through Material Parameter Estimation

Debris Impact Test on Titanium Alloy Plate

Abinash Patro and Ala Tabiei

Department of Mechanical Engineering, University of Cincinnati
College of Engineering and Applied Science

Under Review in Journal of Dynamic Behavior of Materials, 2025

Abstract

Mesh size is crucial in finite element analysis (FEA) simulations, and this study emphasizes mesh regularization to eliminate mesh dependency. Mesh regularization ensures that the numerical results align with the experimental data across different mesh sizes and reduces computational time. Current material-model-based scaling factor methods rely on trial and error, which is inefficient. To address this, the authors introduced four mathematical models to predict scaling factors based on material failure criteria and mesh size, focusing on the Johnson-Cook material model. In this study, mesh regularization was used for impact tests on TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V) titanium alloy plates. Scaling factors typically derived from tensile testing are used to expedite and streamline the simulation process. The authors tested 11 different mesh sizes for both the experimentally acquired and mathematically predicted scaling factors, which were then compared. Applying predictive scaling factors to the impact tests reduced the residual velocity deviation from 17.81% to 1.67% and the maximum deviation in energy absorption from 48.40% to 8.10% compared to tests without scaling factors. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of predictive scaling factors in enhancing test accuracy. This study presents a novel mathematical approach for predicting material-model-based scaling factors for mesh regularization and discusses its potential to reduce simulation time across various problems.

Objective

The primary objective of this research was to develop a predictive methodology for determining mesh-size-based scaling factors that eliminate mesh dependency in finite element analysis of ductile failure. Traditional approaches rely on trial-and-error methods to establish these scaling factors, which is time-consuming and computationally expensive.

This study aimed to:

  • Develop theoretical models to predict scaling factors based on material properties and mesh size
  • Validate the predictive models through tensile testing of Ti-6Al-4V specimens
  • Apply the methodology to debris impact tests on titanium alloy plates
  • Demonstrate significant reduction in mesh-dependent errors for both residual velocity and energy absorption predictions

Methodology

The research employed a comprehensive approach combining theoretical modeling, numerical simulation, and experimental validation:

  • Define the material damage model (*MAT_224 Tabulated Johnson-Cook) used for mesh regularization
  • Develop four theoretical models to determine scaling factors: Fracture Strain Equation Approach, Normalizing Young's Modulus Equation, Fracture Energy Equation Approach, and Continuum Damage Energy Approach
  • Define material properties for Ti-6Al-4V titanium alloy
  • Perform numerical simulations of tensile test specimens with 11 different mesh sizes (0.4mm to 2.0mm)
  • Compare experimental scaling factors with theoretically predicted scaling factors
  • Apply both experimental and theoretical scale factors to debris impact problem

Theoretical Models Evaluated

1. Fracture Strain Equation Approach

This approach combines fracture strain with swift diffuse necking conditions. The model accounts for element thickness-to-length ratio and stress triaxiality effects on failure strain.

2. Normalizing Young's Modulus Equation

This approach normalizes Young's modulus based on mesh size and cohesive stiffness, providing a simplified scaling relationship.

3. Fracture Energy Equation Approach

This model relates fracture energy to stress-strain behavior and element size through an exponential microcrack propagation function.

4. Continuum Damage Energy and Local Fracture Energy Approach

The most successful model, separating fracture energy into continuum (strain-hardening) and local (strain-softening) components. This approach provided the closest predictions to experimental scaling factors, with maximum deviation of only 1.67%.

Tensile Test Findings

Eleven different mesh sizes were tested ranging from 0.4 mm to 2.0 mm, with a base mesh size of 0.7 mm established through comparison with experimental data. The scaling factors obtained showed an inversely proportional relationship with mesh size.

Debris Impact Test Findings

In the debris impact test on TC4 plates, the experimental scaling factors reduced the maximum difference in residual velocity and energy absorption to 4-5% from the base mesh size, compared to 17-18% error without scaling factors. When theoretically predicted scaling factors were applied, the error was further reduced to 1.67%.

Conclusions

  • A new predictive methodology for mesh regularization was successfully developed and validated for isotropic materials
  • The Continuum Damage Energy approach provided the most accurate predictions among the four theoretical models tested
  • Mesh regularization through material-model-based scaling factors dramatically reduced mesh-dependent errors in both tensile and impact simulations
  • The methodology reduces the need for extensive calibration testing across multiple mesh sizes, significantly decreasing computational time and experimental effort
  • Scaling factors showed consistent inverse proportionality with mesh size across all test cases
  • The approach is applicable to various impact and failure problems involving isotropic ductile materials

Significance & Impact

This research provides a practical roadmap for engineers and researchers conducting finite element simulations of material failure and erosion. The predictive methodology eliminates the trial-and-error approach traditionally used for mesh regularization, offering:

  • Reduced computational costs through smaller, more efficient test problems
  • Improved accuracy in predicting failure behavior across different mesh resolutions
  • Enhanced reliability of crashworthiness and impact simulations in aerospace and automotive industries
  • A validated framework applicable to various impact and penetration scenarios
