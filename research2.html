Effect of Ply Orientation and Triaxiality on Mesh Regularization for Carbon/Epoxy Composites

Through Material Parameter Estimation

Abinash Patro and Ala Tabiei

Department of Mechanical and Materials Engineering
College of Engineering and Applied Science, University of Cincinnati
2901 Woodside Drive, Cincinnati, OH 45221, USA

Submitted to Journal Not Specified, 2025
Version: August 18, 2025

Abstract

The mesh size significantly affects the accuracy and computational efficiency of finite-element analysis (FEA) simulations. This study investigates mesh regularization to mitigate mesh dependency, align numerical results with experimental data, and optimize the computational time for carbon/epoxy composites.

Mesh regularization was implemented using the *MAT_ADD_GENERALIZED_DAMAGE (MAGD) model in LS-DYNA, which incorporates a scaling factor based on the ply orientation and stress triaxiality to adjust the material failure criterion. To address the limitations of trial-and-error methods for determining scaling factors, four analytical models were developed to predict these factors as functions of element size.

These predictions were validated against experimentally derived scaling factors for unidirectional carbon/epoxy composites across three ply orientations (0°, 45°, and 90°) and three stress triaxiality conditions (tension, compression, and shear) using mesh sizes ranging from 0.5 mm to 1.5 mm. The scaling factor effectively reduced the mesh dependency in the tested configurations.

A clear relationship between ply orientation and mesh regularization was established; however, no definitive correlation was observed with stress triaxiality. Among the theoretical approaches, the stress degradation model yielded the most consistent predictions, although discrepancies with the experimental results indicate the need for further refinement.

The Mesh Dependency Problem

Finite element analysis (FEA) is often sensitive to mesh configurations employed in computational models. Although finer meshes tend to improve accuracy, particularly in regions with steep gradients or complex geometries, they also lead to increased computational costs. This creates a critical trade-off:

Fine Meshes
Higher Accuracy, Higher Cost
Coarse Meshes
Lower Cost, Reduced Precision
The Challenge
Balance Accuracy & Efficiency

Why Mesh Regularization Matters

Experimental evidence confirms that the fracture behavior in materials is influenced by element size. Even fine, uniform meshes can yield non-convergent results. The process of eliminating mesh-size dependency is referred to as mesh regularization.

  • Mesh-induced influences on results are undesirable because they stem from numerical artifacts rather than physical behavior
  • Particularly critical in dynamic simulations involving impact, wave propagation, and crashworthiness
  • Excessively fine, unregularized elements can distort results and inflate calculated energy dissipation

Methodology

This section outlines the mesh regularization procedure employed in the finite element analysis (FEA) of composite materials using the LS-DYNA code. The primary objective is to establish a systematic approach for determining the scaling factors to mitigate mesh dependency in FEA simulations.

  • Definition of the material damage model used for mesh regularization (*MAT_54 and *MAT_ADD_GENERALIZED_DAMAGE)
  • Development of four theoretical models to predict scaling factors as functions of mesh size
  • Specification of material properties for unidirectional carbon fiber/epoxy composite (65% fiber volume fraction)
  • Execution of numerical simulations on specimens subjected to different stress triaxiality conditions (tensile, compressive, and shear tests)
  • Comparison of experimentally derived scaling factors with analytically predicted values to evaluate validity and applicability

Material Models in LS-DYNA

*MAT_54 (Enhanced Composite Damage)

Provides a robust framework for capturing the linear elastic behavior and initial failure of unidirectional composites. The model accounts for:

  • Progressive failure of laminated fiber-reinforced composites
  • Distinct failure modes: fiber tension/compression, matrix tension/compression, and in-plane shear
  • Strain-rate sensitivity and element erosion

Linear Orthotropic Elasticity

σ₁ = E₁ × ε₁ (Fiber Direction)
σ₂ = E₂ × ε₂ (Matrix Direction)
τ₁₂ = G₁₂ × γ₁₂ (In-Plane Shear)

*MAT_ADD_GENERALIZED_DAMAGE (MAGD)

Enhances the formulation by incorporating advanced damage evolution features:

  • Mesh size regularization through scaling factors
  • Stress triaxiality dependence
  • Ply orientation consideration
  • Strain-rate effects
  • Second-order anisotropic damage tensor

Damage Tensor Components

For shell elements modeling composites, the in-plane damage behavior is simplified:

  • D₁₁: Damage accumulation along 0° fiber direction
  • D₂₂: Damage in 90° direction
  • D₄₄: Damage associated with 45° ply orientation

The material used in this study was a unidirectional carbon fiber/epoxy composite with a fiber volume fraction of 65%. The mechanical and failure properties were determined using a Gr/Ep coupon test.

Four Theoretical Models for Scaling Factors

1
Fracture Energy Equation Approach

Based on Kwak et al.'s work on distribution of microcracks and fracture energy. Uses exponential function to describe microcrack development within elements.

2
Continuum Damage Energy & Local Fracture Energy

Divides fracture energy into continuum damage (strain-hardening) and local fracture (strain-softening) portions with size effect function.

3
Modified Elastic Modulus Approach

Based on Feng et al.'s work, defines mesh regularization based on total strain energy with regularization parameter to eliminate mesh dependency.

4
Stress Degradation Approach

Shyamsunder et al.'s method relating failure strain to stress degradation from fracture strain energy, accounting for characteristic element length.

General Scaling Factor Formula

i(l꜀, τ) = ε₀_mesh_size / ε₀_base_mesh_size

Where the base mesh size is determined by aligning numerical simulation results with experimental data.

Key Findings

Scaling Factor Behavior

  • A mesh size of 0.5 mm consistently served as the base mesh across all ply orientations and triaxiality conditions
  • Scaling factors demonstrated an inverse relationship with mesh size (factor decreased as mesh size increased)
  • Clear relationship established between ply orientation and mesh regularization
  • No definitive correlation observed with stress triaxiality

Relative Error Percentage (REP) Analysis

To quantify model accuracy, REP was calculated as:

REP = (SF_exp - SF_pre) / SF_exp × 100
REP Range
1.53% - 54.10%
Tensile & Shear
Lower REP (Better)
Compression
Higher REP (More Error)

Why Compression Tests Show Higher Error

  • Complex failure mechanisms: localized buckling, matrix microcracking, fiber kinking
  • Significant nonlinearity and heterogeneity in the failure process
  • Attenuation of vibrational energy in explicit dynamic simulations
  • Simplified representations of frictional contact between composite plies
  • Stress waves generating transient triaxial stress states

Best Performing Model

Stress Degradation Model

Among the four theoretical predictive models evaluated, the stress degradation model yielded the most consistent correlation with experimental results.

εf_ii = ε₀_ii + (2G) / (σ₀_ii × L)

Scaling factor derived from this model:

i(l꜀, τ) = [ε₀_ii + 2G/(σ₀_ii × L_base)] / [ε₀_ii + 2G/(σ₀_ii × L)]

Where:

  • εf_ii = failure strain in ii direction
  • ε₀_ii = yield failure in ii direction
  • G = Fracture energy
  • L = Characteristic element length
  • σ₀_ii = stress in ii direction

Model Limitations

Despite being the best performer, the stress degradation model still failed to fully capture:

  • Irregularities observed in experimental data
  • Influence of mesh discretization techniques (element shape, orientation, transition effects)
  • Complex interactions in high-to-low loading compression scenarios

Comparison Across Loading Conditions

Tensile Tests (τ = 0.33)

Scaling factors showed consistent decreasing trend with increasing mesh size across all ply orientations (0°, 45°, 90°). Best match with experimental data achieved using 0.5 mm base mesh.

Compression Tests (τ = -0.33)

General decreasing trend observed for 45° and 90° orientations. Notable exception: 0° ply orientation showed deviation from expected trend, indicating complex failure mechanisms under compression.

Shear Tests (τ = 0.0)

Similar decreasing trend across all ply orientations. Relatively consistent behavior compared to compression tests.

Key Observation

When comparing scaling factors across all test types and ply orientations, no clear or consistent trend could be identified for stress triaxiality effects. This indicates the complex and material-specific nature of mesh sensitivity in composite failure modeling.

Factors Contributing to Mesh Sensitivity

The observed mesh dependency may be attributed to several factors:

Element Formulation

While element type itself doesn't induce mesh dependency, it contributes to mesh sensitivity and affects how damage localizes within the mesh.

Mesh Discretization

Combination of quadrilateral and triangular shell elements may contribute to irregularities in damage propagation and mesh sensitivity.

Interpolation Functions

Approximation functions used in finite element analysis affect the accuracy of stress and strain distributions.

Integration Scheme

Numerical integration methods employed influence the computed element response and damage accumulation.

Energy-Based Regularization Limitations

Energy-based regularization methods fail to fully mitigate mesh-dependent damage localization. Element formulations can influence how damage localizes within the mesh, potentially affecting the global force-displacement response of the structure.

Alternative Approaches: Adaptive Meshing

An alternative to fixed-mesh regularization strategies is adaptive meshing or remeshing, which enables localized refinement based on evolving solution fields.

Energy-Based Adaptive Refinement

The core principle is to equidistribute the discretization error (measured in energy norm) across all elements, minimizing global error in strain energy between numerical and exact solutions.

Robust Error Estimators

  • Zienkiewicz-Zhu (ZZ) Estimator: Superconvergent patch recovery technique
  • Residual-Based Estimators: Use equilibrium equation residuals
  • Goal-Oriented Estimators: Focus refinement on specific quantities of interest
  • Equilibrated Flux Estimators: Based on stress field equilibration
  • Element Energy-Dissipation Methods: Track energy dissipation for refinement

Adaptive Meshing Challenges

  • Significant computational overhead from repeated mesh updates
  • Effectiveness highly dependent on error estimator precision
  • Mesh distortion in large deformation problems requiring frequent remeshing
  • May affect numerical stability and convergence
  • Limitations in multiphysics simulations
  • Difficulties with complex contact conditions
  • Challenges with highly anisotropic material behavior

Failure Criteria: Chang-Chang Model

This study employed the Chang-Chang failure criterion to model composite failure. For fiber-dominated laminated composites under tensile loading, in-plane failure occurs in three distinct modes:

Matrix Cracking

Failure in the matrix material between fibers

Fiber Breakage

Tensile or compressive failure of reinforcing fibers

Fiber-Matrix Shear

Shear failure at the fiber-matrix interface

Matrix Cracking Criterion

For linear elastic laminates:

(σy/Yt)² + (σxy/Sc)² = (ef_m)²

Where failure occurs when ef_m ≥ 1

Fiber Failure Criterion

Modified Yamada-Sun criterion for fiber breakage and fiber-matrix shearing:

(σx/Xt)² + (σxy/Sc)² = (ef_f)²

Where failure occurs when ef_f ≥ 1

Damage Parameter Evolution

After defining failure strain, damage parameters for different fiber orientations:

d_ii = εii / εf_ii

Three distinct damage components (D₁₁, D₂₂, D₄₄) evolve according to:

Ḋ = n × D^(1-1/n) × ḋii × i(l꜀, τ)

Conclusions

Major Findings

  • Comprehensive investigation of ply orientation and stress triaxiality influence on mesh regularization in orthotropic composites
  • Mesh regularization successfully implemented using scaling factors in MAGD framework within LS-DYNA
  • Carbon/epoxy laminate analyzed across three ply orientations (0°, 45°, 90°) and three loading conditions
  • Generally inverse relationship between scaling factor and mesh size observed
  • Base mesh size of 0.5 mm identified as optimal across all configurations

Model Performance

  • Four theoretical models evaluated for predicting scaling factors based on mesh size
  • Stress degradation model produced most consistent results across different ply orientations and test configurations
  • Strong correlation between ply orientation and mesh regularization identified
  • No conclusive trend established for influence of stress triaxiality on mesh regularization behavior
  • REP values ranged from 1.53% to 54.10%, with better performance in tensile/shear vs. compression tests

Practical Implications

The approach presented—introducing mesh-dependent scaling factors into the material model—offers a practical and effective strategy for mitigating mesh sensitivity in finite element simulations of orthotropic materials.

However, existing theoretical models remain inadequate for fully capturing mesh regularization effects in orthotropic composites, particularly under varied triaxial loading conditions.

Future Research Directions

Model Development Needs

  • Further development of predictive models accounting for material anisotropy
  • Better incorporation of mesh discretization effects
  • Enhanced modeling of multiaxial failure mechanisms
  • Integration of load history and sequence effects
  • Improved representation of exponential energy release during crack formation

Experimental Validation

  • More extensive testing across wider range of mesh sizes
  • Additional ply orientations beyond 0°, 45°, 90°
  • More complex loading conditions and stress triaxiality states
  • Different composite material systems (glass fiber, aramid, etc.)
  • Temperature and environmental effects on scaling factors

Advanced Techniques

  • Probabilistic framework incorporating material property variability
  • Machine learning approaches for scaling factor prediction
  • Multiscale modeling bridging micro and macro scales
  • Coupling with adaptive meshing strategies
  • Integration with digital twin concepts for real-time predictions

Improved Accuracy
