Spurious Element Failure of Cohesive Element under Impact

Modified Bilinear Traction Separation Law for Dynamic Loading

Based on Fras et al. Research Paper

French Bullet Penetration Study - AA7020-T651 Plates

Authors: T. Fras, L. Colard, E. Lach, A. Rusinek, B. Reck

Abstract

This research addresses a critical limitation in cohesive element modeling under dynamic impact conditions. Traditional bilinear traction separation laws fail to capture crack propagation behavior between material layers when subjected to ballistic impact, particularly in cases where separation at failure differs significantly between normal and tangential directions.

The study investigates thick AA7020-T651 aluminum alloy plates under ballistic impact from fragment-simulating projectiles. Original simulations using MAT_Erosion with standard cohesive elements demonstrated significant discrepancies from experimental data, failing to capture inter-layer crack formation observed in physical tests.

A modified bilinear traction separation law is proposed that accounts for loading ratio history, preventing spurious element failure caused by sudden changes in loading conditions. The modified formulation has been validated against experimental data from bullet penetration tests and Kalthoff impact experiments, demonstrating superior accuracy in predicting crack propagation and energy absorption characteristics.

Reasons for Element Failure

In the traditional bilinear traction separation law, damage is calculated through mean separation. However, critical issues arise in materials where there is a large difference between separation at failure in the normal direction and separation at failure in the tangential direction.

Root Cause Analysis

When a sudden change in loading ratio occurs, the traditional bilinear law can lead to premature element failure even when the mean separation remains constant. This phenomenon is particularly problematic under dynamic impact conditions where loading ratios change rapidly.

Loading Ratio Change Leading to Sudden Element Failure

In cases of materials with large differences in separation to failure between normal and tangential directions, sudden changes in loading ratio can lead to element failure even for the same mean separation. This fundamental limitation of the traditional approach necessitates a new formulation that considers loading change history.

Modified Bilinear Traction Separation Law

The proposed modified bilinear traction separation law introduces consideration of loading ratio history, addressing the spurious failure issue while maintaining thermodynamic consistency.

Key Innovation

The modified law evaluates damage accumulation by considering both current loading conditions and loading history. When the loading ratio (beta) changes, the total separation for failure is reevaluated based on accumulated damage history and loading ratio history.

Mean Separation for Damage Initiation and Failure

Conditions and Formulations
If sₙ ≠ 0 and βⁱ ≠ βⁱ⁻¹:

sm₀ = sn₀ × st₀ × sqrt((1 + β²) / ((β × sn₀)² + st₀²))

smf_new = sm + 2(1 + β²) / sm₀ / (EN/GIC + β² × ET/GIIC)

sn₀ = sm₀ / sqrt(1 + β²)

snf_new = smf_new / sqrt(1 + β²)
β = loading ratio (Normal/Tangential)
sm₀ = separation for damage initiation
smf_new = separation for complete failure
sn₀ = separation for damage initiation (normal)
snf_new = separation for complete failure (normal)
EN = initial stiffness (normal direction)
ET = initial stiffness (tangential direction)
GIC = critical energy release rate (Mode I)
GIIC = critical energy release rate (Mode II)

Tangential Separation for Damage Initiation and Failure

Tangential Directions Formulation
st1₀ = β × sm₀ / sqrt(1 + β²) / sqrt(1 + γ²)

st1f_new = β × smf_new / sqrt(1 + β²) / sqrt(1 + γ²)

st2₀ = γ × β × sm₀ / sqrt(1 + β²) / sqrt(1 + γ²)

st2f_new = γ × β × smf_new / sqrt(1 + β²) / sqrt(1 + γ²)
γ = tangential loading ratio (t₁/t₂)
st1₀ = separation initiation (tangential 1)
st1f_new = separation failure (tangential 1)
st2₀ = separation initiation (tangential 2)
st2f_new = separation failure (tangential 2)

Damage Accumulation

Damage Calculation
dm_new = smf_new × (sm - sm₀) / sm / (smf_new - sm₀)

Constraints:
if dm_new < 0: dm_new = 0
if dm_new > 1: dm_new = 1
dm_new = total damage accumulated
sm = mean separation

Key Mechanism

In the modified bilinear law, when the loading ratio (beta) changes, the total separation for failure is reevaluated based on the total damage accumulated history and loading ratio history. This dynamic adjustment prevents premature element failure while maintaining physical consistency.

Advantages of Modified Formulation

Technical Improvements

  • Loading History Consideration: Tracks and accounts for changes in loading ratio over time
  • Spurious Failure Prevention: Eliminates non-physical element failures due to loading direction changes
  • Dynamic Reevaluation: Continuously updates failure criteria based on accumulated damage
  • Physical Consistency: Maintains thermodynamic principles throughout loading history
  • Improved Accuracy: Superior correlation with experimental data across multiple test cases

Conclusions

Research Achievements

  • Successfully identified and characterized spurious element failure mechanism in traditional cohesive element formulations under dynamic loading
  • Developed a modified bilinear traction separation law that incorporates loading ratio history to prevent non-physical failures
  • Validated the proposed formulation against experimental data from bullet penetration tests and Kalthoff impact experiments
  • Demonstrated significant improvements in crack propagation prediction, energy absorption accuracy, and damage pattern representation

Significance and Impact

This research provides a fundamentally improved approach for modeling cohesive element behavior under dynamic impact conditions. The modified formulation addresses a critical limitation in existing methods by preventing spurious failures while maintaining computational efficiency and physical consistency. The validation against multiple experimental configurations demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the approach.
