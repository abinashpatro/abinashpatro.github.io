Elastic-Plastic Bilinear Traction Separation Law Through Fatigue Crack Growth Rate for Adhesive Joints

A Novel Approach for Low Cycle Fatigue Analysis

Research Presentation

Based on Elastic-Plastic Bilinear Traction Separation Law Research

Presented at Advanced Materials Conference, 2024

Abstract

This research presents a new elastic-plastic bilinear traction separation law for modeling fatigue crack growth in adhesive joints under low cycle fatigue conditions. Traditional approaches often fail to accurately predict material behavior in low cycle fatigue where both elastic and plastic deformations contribute significantly to material failure.

The proposed methodology establishes elastic and plastic separation components for the bilinear traction-separation law based on flow rule principles. By applying thermodynamic consistency principles, we redefine the energy release rate for low-cycle fatigue models, leading to the development of a new damage accumulation model.

The model has been rigorously verified against three distinct experimental cases, demonstrating improved predictive capability compared to traditional high cycle fatigue approaches that tend to predict premature failure when compared to experimental data.

Introduction

High Cycle Fatigue (HCF)
  • Low stress levels
  • Primarily linear material behavior
  • Material failure depends on yield stress
  • Predicts premature failure compared to experimental data
Low Cycle Fatigue (LCF)
  • Higher stress levels
  • Both elastic and plastic deformation
  • Complex material deformation behavior
  • Requires advanced modeling approaches

Research Objective

This research addresses the limitations of traditional fatigue models by proposing an elastic-plastic bilinear traction separation law that accurately captures material behavior in low cycle fatigue conditions for adhesive joints.

Methodology

Proposed Approach

  • Establish elastic and plastic separation for bilinear traction-separation law based on flow rule
  • Apply thermodynamic consistency principles to redefine energy release rate
  • Develop new damage accumulation model for low-cycle fatigue
  • Validate model against experimental data from three distinct cases

  • Applicability to various joint configurations

Elastic-Plastic Energy Release Rate

Energy release rate defines crack propagation in Paris Law, making it crucial to define an accurate elastic-plastic energy release rate for low cycle fatigue modeling.

Thermodynamic Consistency Principles

  • First Law of Thermodynamics: Total energy remains constant, even when converted between forms. Introduced through Helmholtz free energy density formulation.
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics: Energy transfer leads to dissipation. Maintained through energy dissipation formulation.

Mathematical Formulation

The elastic-plastic energy release rate is derived through rigorous application of thermodynamic principles, ensuring consistency with both energy conservation and dissipation laws.

Conclusion

Research Achievements

  • Successfully established elastic-plastic separation for bilinear traction-separation law based on flow rule
  • Applied laws of thermodynamics to redefine energy release rate for low-cycle fatigue model
  • Developed a new damage accumulation model with formulations for pure and mixed-mode loading
  • Verified the proposed model against three distinct experimental cases with improved accuracy

Significance and Impact

This research provides a fundamentally improved approach for modeling fatigue crack growth in adhesive joints under low cycle fatigue conditions. The thermodynamically consistent formulation and explicit separation of elastic and plastic components represent significant advancements over traditional methods.

Future Research Directions

Potential Extensions

  • Multi-scale Modeling: Bridge micro-scale damage mechanisms with macro-scale structural behavior
  • Temperature Effects: Incorporate thermal influences on elastic-plastic separation
  • Rate Dependency: Extend model to account for loading rate effects
  • Material Variability: Develop probabilistic framework for material property variations
  • Complex Loading Spectra: Apply to realistic service loading conditions

Application Areas

  • Aerospace structural joints
  • Automotive bonded components
  • Wind turbine blade adhesives
  • Civil infrastructure reinforcement
  • Marine composite structures
