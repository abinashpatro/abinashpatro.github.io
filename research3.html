Fatigue Failure of Adhesive Joints in Fiber-Reinforced Composite Material Under Step/Variable Amplitude Loading

A Critical Literature Review

Department of Mechanical and Materials Engineering
College of Engineering and Applied Science, University of Cincinnati

Published in Journal of Composites Science, 2024, 8, 477
DOI: 10.3390/jcs8110477

Abstract

Most fatigue-loading research has concentrated on constant-amplitude tests, which seldom represent actual service conditions. Because of the significant time and expense associated with variable-amplitude experiments, researchers often employ block/step-loading tests to evaluate the effects of variable-amplitude loading. These tests utilize various sequences of low-to-high and high-to-low loads to simulate real-world scenarios.

Empirical investigations have shown inconsistencies in the damage accumulation under different load sequences. Although literature reviews exist for simulation and experimental methods, there is limited research examining the impact of step/variable-amplitude loading on adhesive joints in composite materials.

This review aims to address this gap by comprehensively analyzing the effects of load sequence and block loading on fatigue damage progression in fiber-reinforced polymer composites. Additionally, the applicability of various step-loading fatigue damage accumulation models to adhesive materials is evaluated through numerical simulation to study its suitability in predicting fatigue failure.

This review also explores recent theoretical advancements in this field over the past few years, examining more than 100 fatigue damage accumulation models categorized into seven subcategories. Finally, numerical simulations using the most common nonlinear cumulative fatigue damage accumulation models were conducted to predict fatigue failure in adhesively bonded joints under four step-loading tests, and the results were compared with the experimental data.

The Problem

Adhesive joints under fatigue in real-life service usually encounter variable/step loading and rarely encounter a constant amplitude. However, fatigue testing is often performed at a constant amplitude owing to:

  • High cost and variable-amplitude experimental time
  • Fatigue testing facility limitations
  • The unclear nature of the in-service loading spectrum

Key Challenges

Several experimental investigations have uncovered notable inconsistencies between empirical results and conventional models that assume uniform fatigue damage accumulation. The observed discrepancies can be attributed to:

  • Neglecting to factor in the load history
  • Inability to consider the loading sequence when determining fatigue failure
  • Disregarding the impact of load interaction effects

Miner's Rule & Its Limitations

In 1945, Palmgren and Miner introduced a mathematical model to address cumulative fatigue damage under variable loading, expressed as:

Miner's Linear Damage Rule (LDR)

D = Σ (ni / Ni)

Where:

  • D is the total damage
  • ni is the number of fatigue cycles under the ith constant-amplitude loading level
  • Ni is the number of fatigue cycles until failure under the ith constant-amplitude loading level

Loading Sequence Effects

Miner's rule typically yields:

  • Conservative predictions for low-to-high loading sequences (D > 1)
  • Non-conservative predictions for high-to-low loading sequences (D < 1)

Why Does This Happen?

  • Substantial strain often leads to early development of initial microscopic cracks that expand under less intense cycles
  • Increased load cycles cause surface roughening during repeated plastic deformation, generating additional crack initiation sites
  • Damage accumulation is not a linear process

Seven Categories of Damage Models

This comprehensive review examined over 100 fatigue damage accumulation models, categorized into seven main groups:

1
Linear Damage Rules

Models based on Palmgren-Miner's linear summation approach, including strain-based variants and modifications for adhesive materials.

2
Nonlinear Damage Curve Models

Including Marco-Starkey NLDR, Double Linear Damage Rule (DLDR), and Damage Curve Approach (DCA) by Manson and Halford.

3
Life Curve Modification Models

Based on Corten-Dolan and Freudenthal-Heller approaches, and iso-damage line concepts by Subramanyan and Hashin-Rotem.

4
Crack Growth Models

Based on LEFM and EPFM theories, including Paris law, Wheeler model, Willenborg-Wood model, and UniGrow approach.

5
Continuum Damage Mechanics

Founded on Kachanov-Rabotnov theory, developed by Chaboche and extended by numerous researchers for various loading conditions.

6
Material Degradation Models

Based on material property changes during fatigue, including Ye-Wang model and material memory concepts.

7
Energy-Based Models

Using strain energy density approaches, including Ellyin's total energy concept and various hysteresis energy models.

Key Findings

  • Most models showed lower error than Miner's rule for low-to-high sequences
  • Low error in low-to-high cases due to smaller post-transition cycle counts
  • All models exhibited substantial errors in high-to-low sequences
  • Radhakrishnan's model performed best for high-to-low cases
  • High-to-low sequences show crack growth retardation effects
  • Low-to-high sequences show crack growth acceleration

Model Limitations Identified

Critical Deficiencies

The simulation results revealed that most existing fatigue failure models are insufficient for accurately predicting fatigue failure in adhesive joints due to:

  • Neglect of load history: Many models fail to account for the cumulative effects of previous loading events
  • Inadequate sequence consideration: Loading order significantly impacts damage but is often ignored
  • Missing load interaction effects: The influence of one load cycle on subsequent cycles is not captured
  • Linear damage assumption: Most models assume steady, linear failure progression
  • Energy release rate oversight: Exponential increase in energy release during crack formation is not modeled
  • Crack propagation simplification: The swift and progressive nature of crack growth during fatigue is inadequately represented

Notable Model Approaches

1. Damage Curve Approach (DCA)

Developed by Manson and Halford, this model addresses the nonlinear nature of damage accumulation:

  • Uses power relationship between damage and cycle ratio
  • Incorporates load-level dependency through exponent α
  • DCA showed 12% average overestimation vs. 37% for Miner's rule
  • Accounts for loading sequence effects in multi-level loading

2. Continuum Damage Mechanics (CDM)

Based on Chaboche's work, linking damage to physical material degradation:

  • Considers damage growth below initial fatigue threshold
  • Incorporates strain hardening variable for interaction effects
  • Directly connects damage to mean stress
  • Material parameters easily determined from standard S-N curves
  • Multiple variants developed for different loading conditions

3. UniGrow Model

Noroozi's elastic-plastic fatigue crack growth model:

  • Based on two-parameter driving force (Kmax, ΔK)
  • Accounts for elastic-plastic stress/strain near crack tip
  • Considers plasticity-induced crack closure effects
  • Applicable to both short and long crack regimes
  • Successfully validated for various aluminum alloys

4. Energy-Based Models

Ellyin's total energy concept combining elastic and plastic components:

  • Addresses both high-cycle and low-cycle fatigue
  • Uses total strain energy density (ΔWt = ΔWe + ΔWp)
  • Creates master curve analogous to S-N curve
  • Applicable to both Masing and non-Masing materials
  • Accounts for crack initiation and propagation

Conclusions

Key Findings

  • Over 100 cumulative fatigue damage accumulation models were reviewed across seven major categories
  • Most models performed adequately for low-to-high load sequences but poorly for high-to-low sequences
  • Numerical simulations using double-lap joint specimens revealed significant limitations in current models
  • High-to-low loading cases showed crack growth retardation effects not captured by existing models
  • Energy release rate increases exponentially during crack formation, but most models assume linear progression

Critical Gaps Identified

  • Lack of comprehensive load history consideration in damage calculations
  • Insufficient modeling of load sequence effects on fatigue life
  • Inadequate representation of load interaction phenomena
  • Limited ability to capture rapid crack propagation during final failure stages
  • Poor performance predicting high-to-low loading scenarios for adhesive joints

Research Needs

Additional research is necessary to:

  • Develop improved failure models incorporating all critical factors (load history, sequence, interactions)
  • Better represent the swift and progressive nature of crack propagation
  • Account for exponential increase in energy release rate during failure
  • Provide more reliable predictions for adhesive joints under variable loading
  • Validate models specifically for fiber-reinforced polymer composite joints

Future Directions

Recommended Model Development Paths

  • Hybrid Approaches: Combine strengths of different model categories (e.g., CDM with crack growth concepts)
  • Machine Learning Integration: Use data-driven methods to capture complex load interaction effects
  • Multiscale Modeling: Bridge microscale damage mechanisms with macroscale structural behavior
  • Enhanced Energy Methods: Develop energy-based approaches specifically for adhesive materials
  • Probabilistic Framework: Account for variability in material properties and loading conditions

Experimental Validation Needs

  • More comprehensive variable amplitude testing of adhesive joints
  • In-situ damage monitoring during step-loading experiments
  • Characterization of crack growth rates under various load sequences
  • Temperature and environmental effects on fatigue damage accumulation
  • Long-term durability studies under realistic service spectra

Significance & Impact

This comprehensive literature review provides valuable insights for researchers and engineers working on:

  • Aerospace Applications: Aircraft structural joints subjected to complex flight load spectra
  • Automotive Industry: Adhesively bonded composite components under variable road loads
  • Wind Energy: Turbine blade adhesive joints experiencing fluctuating wind loads
  • Civil Infrastructure: Bridge and building components with composite reinforcement
  • Marine Structures: Ships and offshore platforms with bonded joints under wave loading

Practical Implications

  • Guides selection of appropriate damage models for specific loading scenarios
  • Highlights critical limitations in current predictive methodologies
  • Identifies research gaps requiring further investigation
  • Provides framework for developing next-generation fatigue models
  • Supports safer, more efficient design of adhesively bonded structures

Key Takeaways for Practice

  • No single model adequately addresses all aspects of variable amplitude fatigue
  • Loading sequence has profound impact on fatigue life that must be considered
  • High-to-low sequences are particularly challenging to predict accurately
  • Combination of multiple model types may be necessary for comprehensive predictions
  • Experimental validation remains essential for critical applications
