Adhesive Joints in Fiber-Reinforced Composite Materials Under Step/Variable-Amplitude Loading

Abinash Patro and Ala Tabiei

Department of Mechanical and Materials Engineering

Published in Journal of Composites Science, 2025
DOI: 10.3390/jcs9030112

Abstract

Adhesive joints in real-world conditions often experience variable or step loading rather than constant-amplitude fatigue. This study addresses this gap by examining the influence of load sequence and block loading on fatigue damage in adhesive joints of fiber-reinforced polymer (FRP) composites.

A novel bilinear traction-separation law based on the Fatigue Crack Growth Rate (FCGR) rule is introduced to predict fatigue failure under step/variable loads, accounting for load history, sequence, and interaction effects. This model was validated using a double-lap joint model under step/variable loading across four experimental scenarios.

The proposed model outperformed existing fatigue damage-accumulation models, significantly reducing the Relative Error of Prediction (REP). Notably, the proposed model achieved reductions from 81.10% to as low as 0.013% in certain cases. The proposed bilinear law exhibited an accelerated damage accumulation rate per cycle for low-to-high loading situations and a decelerated rate for high-to-low loading scenarios, aligning more closely with experimental observations.

The proposed model offers practical benefits by improving fatigue life predictions, enabling optimized FRP composite designs, and minimizing overengineering. These advancements are particularly relevant in industries such as aerospace, automotive, and wind energy, where structural durability and safety are paramount.

The Challenge of Variable Loading

In real-world scenarios, adhesive joints experience complex loading patterns that vary over time, including changes in load magnitude, frequency, and duration. These variable or step-loading patterns represent actual service conditions more accurately than the constant-amplitude loading typically used in laboratory fatigue tests.

Limitations of Traditional Approaches

Although constant-amplitude fatigue testing is simpler and more economical, it fails to fully capture the intricacies of real-world loading conditions. This discrepancy occurs because constant-amplitude tests do not consider:

  • The effects of load interactions and sequences, which can significantly affect fatigue life
  • Load history that influences subsequent damage accumulation
  • Stress cycles of varying amplitude that occur in actual service conditions
  • The nonlinear nature of damage accumulation under variable loading

Problems with Miner's Rule

The Palmgren-Miner rule, introduced in 1945, represents one of the earliest mathematical approaches to cumulative fatigue damage. Despite its widespread use, experimental results under variable or block loading conditions show considerable differences from Miner's rule predictions.

D = Σ(ni/Ni)

Where D is total damage, ni is cycles at ith load level, Ni is cycles to failure at ith load level

Key Deficiencies of Miner's Rule

  • No Load History: Fails to consider the sequence and history of applied loads
  • No Loading Sequence: Treats all loading sequences as equivalent
  • No Load Interaction: Ignores interactions between different load levels

Observed Behavior in Experiments

Low-to-High Loading (σ₁ < σ₂)
Conservative Predictions
High-to-Low Loading (σ₁ > σ₂)
Non-Conservative Results

For loading sequences from low to high stress, Miner's rule generally produces conservative estimates of fatigue life. However, for sequences from high to low stress, results tend to be non-conservative, potentially unsafe.

Existing Damage Accumulation Models

To address the limitations of Miner's rule, researchers have developed various theories regarding nonlinear damage accumulation. These models can be classified into seven categories:

1
Linear Damage Rules

Palmgren-Miner and similar linear accumulation approaches that assume damage adds linearly.

2
Nonlinear Damage Curve Models

Two-stage linearization models including Marco-Starkey and Manson-Halford approaches.

3
Life Curve Modification Models

Kwofie, Aeran, and Subramanyan models that modify the S-N curve based on loading conditions.

4
Crack Growth Concept Models

Wheeler, Willenborg, and Elber models based on crack closure and plastic zone interactions.

5
Continuum Damage Mechanics

Chaboche and Dattoma models based on continuum mechanics principles for damage evolution.

6
Material Degradation Models

Models considering elastic modulus degradation and material memory effects during fatigue.

7
Energy-Based Models

Feltner-Morrow and hysteresis energy approaches using plastic strain energy as failure criterion.

Persistent Limitations

Despite the variety of approaches, these theories still overlook one or more critical factors: load history, loading sequence effects, and load interaction mechanisms. This study introduces a novel approach that addresses all three factors simultaneously.

Bilinear Traction-Separation Law

Cohesive Zone Model Fundamentals

The cohesive zone model, inspired by the short-crack regime and Paris Law, was introduced by Dugdale and Barenblatt to simulate crack initiation and growth in ductile materials. The bilinear traction-separation law provides a framework for relating fatigue crack growth to damage accumulation.

Damage Accumulation Modes

Normal Separation
Mode II

Original Formulation

The fatigue damage can be characterized using the traction-separation law for different modes:

∂df/∂N = (b/Acz,I)(da/dN) = (32T²)/(9πEGIC)(da/dN)

Mode I: Normal direction damage accumulation

∂df/∂N = (b/Acz,II)(da/dN) = (S²)/(EGIIC)(da/dN)

Mode II: Tangential direction damage accumulation

Paris Law Integration

Fatigue Crack Growth Rate (FCGR) is correlated with the Bilinear Traction-Separation Law through the Paris Law regime:

da/dN = C(ΔG)m

Where C and m are experimentally obtained Paris Law parameters

The Novel Proposed Model

The proposed model introduces an improvised Paris Law that incorporates a new nonlinear load-interaction parameter to the crack propagation rate equation. This addresses the limitations of previous models by accounting for load history, sequence, and interactions.

Key Innovation: Load Interaction Parameter

da/dN = C(ΔGi)m × (Li)vi

Where Li is the load-interaction parameter at the ith level load cycle, and vi is the exponent parameter considering loading history.

Load-Interaction Parameter Definition

For different loading modes, the load-interaction parameter at ith level load cycle is defined as:

Mode Load Interaction Parameter Description
Mode I LI,i = Ti/Ti-1 Normal traction ratio
Mode II LII,i = Si/Si-1 Shear stress ratio
Mixed Mode Li = Tmi/Tmi-1 Combined traction ratio

Loading History Exponent

The exponent vi considers loading history and is linked to material degradation during fatigue failure:

vi = (logNi/logNi-1) × (σi-1/σi)

Based on Corten-Dolan and Morrow's material degradation concepts

Physical Interpretation

This parameter accounts for:

  • Decrease in elastic modulus during fatigue-induced failure
  • Reduction in yield strength as cracks grow under repeated stress cycles
  • Material degradation from declining mobile dislocations
  • Emergence of barriers to dislocation movements
  • Weakening of material toughness throughout the fatigue process

Mathematical Formulation

Enhanced Crack Propagation Equations

The new crack propagation equations for each mode incorporating the load-interaction parameter:

Mode I:
(da/dN)I = C[E∆n²/2(1-R²)(1-ds)(1-df)]m × (Ti/Ti-1)[logNi/logNi-1 × σi-1/σi]
Mode II:
(da/dN)II = C[E∆t²/2(1-R²)(1-ds)(1-df)]m × (Si/Si-1)[logNi/logNi-1 × σi-1/σi]
Mixed Mode:
(da/dN)mm = C[∆GI + β(∆GII - ∆GI)]m × (Tmi/Tmi-1)[logNi/logNi-1 × σi-1/σi]

Behavior Under Different Loading Sequences

Low-to-High Loading

When σ₁ < σ₂ and N₁ > N₂, crack propagation is slower, resulting in MORE cycles to failure than the previous model. Damage accumulates at an accelerated rate per cycle.

High-to-Low Loading

When σ₁ > σ₂ and N₁ < N₂, crack propagates more rapidly, requiring FEWER cycles for fatigue failure than the previous model. Damage accumulates at a decelerated rate per cycle.

Experimental Validation

Empirical evidence from various loading conditions revealed that:

  • In low-to-high loading sequences, cumulative damage D exceeded 1.0
  • In high-to-low loading sequences, cumulative damage D was less than 1.0
  • These trends align with real-world observations and experimental data

Implementation Details

Implicit Time Integration

From a computational standpoint, calculating total fatigue damage cycle-by-cycle for high cycle fatigue (up to ~10⁸ cycles) is extremely expensive. A cyclical jumping technique was utilized:

df,n+m = df,n + j(∂df/∂N)

Where j is the number of cycles in which damage rate is assumed constant

When the cyclic load period ΔT remains constant throughout simulation:

df,i+1 = df,i + (dti/ΔT)(∂df/∂N)

Where i denotes the number of time intervals

Convergence Enhancement

Arc-Length Method

Addresses convergence difficulties at turning points in the material model using LS-DYNA's integrated algorithms.

Viscous Regularization

Introduces viscous damage variable (η = 10⁻⁴) to improve convergence while keeping viscous energy negligible.

dv = ds - η(λ̇/λ)

Viscous damage replaces static damage to enhance stability

LS-DYNA Implementation

An LS-DYNA user material (UMAT) model was created to forecast fatigue failure in adhesive joints within composite materials subjected to varying loads. This model incorporates:

  • Bilinear traction-separation law with FCGR
  • Load-interaction parameters for all three modes
  • Loading history exponent calculation
  • Implicit time integration with cyclic jumping
  • Viscous regularization for convergence (UMAT41c)

Physical Mechanisms

Why Low-to-High Loading Accumulates Damage Faster

Microcrack Initiation

Low stress cycles initiate microscopic cracks that enlarge rapidly under subsequent high stress, causing faster deterioration than constant high stress.

Surface Degradation

Repeated plastic deformation creates surface deterioration, establishing new crack-initiation points that form under fewer cycles at higher stress.

Nonlinear Accumulation

Damage accumulation is not a straightforward linear progression—early damage accelerates subsequent damage at higher loads.

Why High-to-Low Loading Decelerates Damage

Based on Wheeler's crack growth model, the interaction between plastic zones at the crack tip created by residual compressive stresses due to overloading affects subsequent crack growth:

  • High initial loads create large plastic zones at crack tips
  • These zones induce residual compressive stresses
  • Subsequent lower loads must overcome these compressive stresses
  • Crack growth is retarded, extending fatigue life beyond linear predictions

Material Degradation Considerations

The loading history exponent vi accounts for:

  • Decrease in elastic modulus during fatigue failure
  • Reduction in yield strength as cracks propagate
  • Decline in mobile dislocation density
  • Formation of barriers to dislocation movement
  • Progressive weakening of material toughness

Theoretical Significance

Addressing Fundamental Limitations

This research fundamentally addresses the three critical factors that have limited fatigue damage-accumulation models since Miner's rule was introduced in 1945:

1
Load History

The loading history exponent vi = (logNi/logNi-1) × (σi-1/σi) explicitly captures how previous loading affects current damage accumulation.

2
Loading Sequence

The model naturally produces D > 1 for low-to-high sequences and D < 1 for high-to-low sequences, matching experimental observations.

3
Load Interactions

The load-interaction parameter Li = Ti/Ti-1 quantifies how successive load levels interact through crack tip mechanics.

Bridge Between Disciplines

The proposed model uniquely bridges multiple disciplines:

  • Fracture Mechanics: Paris Law and FCGR principles
  • Continuum Damage Mechanics: Damage evolution and traction-separation frameworks
  • Material Science: Degradation mechanisms and dislocation theory
  • Computational Mechanics: Efficient numerical implementation and convergence strategies
  • Experimental Mechanics: Validation against controlled laboratory tests

Paradigm Shift

Unlike previous models that attempt to correct Miner's rule through empirical adjustments, this approach fundamentally reformulates crack propagation rate to include physical mechanisms of load interaction and material memory. This represents a paradigm shift from curve-fitting exercises to mechanistically-based predictions.

Limitations & Considerations

Current Model Limitations

  • Two-Level Loading: Current validation limited to two-block loading; extension to multi-level spectra requires further verification
  • Adhesive-Specific: Paris Law parameters (C, m) must be determined experimentally for each adhesive system
  • Mode Interpolation: Linear interpolation for mixed-mode parameters may oversimplify complex mode interactions
  • Temperature Effects: Current formulation does not explicitly account for temperature-dependent behavior
  • Damage Threshold: Model assumes damage initiates immediately; does not include damage initiation threshold

Practical Implementation Considerations

Material Characterization

Requires FCGR testing to determine Paris Law parameters for Mode I and Mode II. Testing can be time-consuming and requires specialized equipment.

Computational Resources

High-cycle fatigue simulations (10⁶-10⁸ cycles) can require significant computation time even with cyclic jumping technique.

Convergence Sensitivity

Implicit integration with cohesive elements can face convergence challenges; viscous regularization and arc-length methods are essential.

Mesh Dependency

Cohesive element formulation inherently mesh-dependent; element size affects energy dissipation and requires careful mesh design.

Validation Scope

Current validation is based on:

  • Single joint geometry (double-lap configuration)
  • One adhesive system (SikaDur 330)
  • One composite material (GFRP with E-glass/polyester)
  • Four specific loading cases (two low-to-high, two high-to-low)
  • Constant load ratio R = 0.1
  • Room temperature conditions (23°C)

Extrapolation beyond these conditions should be performed cautiously until additional validation is conducted.
